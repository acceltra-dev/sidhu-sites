Know Your Rights

What Is Wills Variation in BC?

British Columbia has some of the strongest wills variation laws in Canada. If a will fails to make adequate provision for a spouse or children, the courts have authority to step in and change it.

Under Section 60 of BC's Wills, Estates and Succession Act (WESA), if a will fails to make adequate provision for the proper maintenance and support of a spouse or children, the court can vary the will and redistribute the estate.

This is not a minor legal technicality. It is a fundamental right that BC law has recognized for over a century. It applies even if the will was properly signed, witnessed, and executed in perfect legal form. It applies even if the deceased left explicit reasons for their choices.

The law acknowledges that testamentary freedom, the right to leave your estate as you choose, must be balanced against the moral and legal obligations that parents and spouses owe to those closest to them.

If you believe you were inadequately provided for, or left out of a will entirely, you may have a legal claim. The deadline to file is 180 days from the date probate is granted. Every day matters.

Section 60, WESA

"The court asks one question: Did the will make adequate provision for the spouse or children? If not, the will can be changed."

Wills, Estates and Succession Act, SBC 2009, c. 13
Eligibility

Who Can Contest a Will in BC?

WESA limits who may bring a wills variation claim, but the categories are broader than most people expect.

S

Spouses & Common-Law Partners

If you were married to the deceased, or lived with them in a marriage-like relationship for at least two years, you qualify as a spouse under WESA and may bring a wills variation claim.

Married or 2+ years together
C

Adult Children

Biological and adopted children of the deceased can challenge an inadequate will, regardless of age. This right does not expire when you turn 18, and estrangement alone does not disqualify you.

Biological and adopted, any age
F

Children of a Former Relationship

Children from a prior marriage or relationship are equally entitled to bring a wills variation claim. A parent's obligation to provide for their children does not end with a new marriage or family.

Prior marriages & relationships
A Common Scenario

When One Child Gets Everything

A parent passes away with an estate worth $4 million. The will leaves everything to one child. The other three children receive nothing. Under BC's wills variation law, those three children have the right to challenge that will in court, and courts regularly redistribute estates in these cases.

"Courts have consistently held that parents have a moral and legal duty to provide for all their children, not just the favoured one."

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Straight answers to the questions our clients ask most often.

How long do I have to contest a will in BC?
180 days from the date probate is granted. This deadline is strict. The courts very rarely grant extensions. Contact us immediately if you believe you have a claim.
Can I contest a will if I was left something, just not enough?
Yes. The question under WESA is whether the amount you received was "adequate" given the size of the estate and your relationship with the deceased, not simply whether you received anything at all. A token bequest from a $3 million estate may well be inadequate.
Does it matter if my parent had reasons for leaving me less?
Courts consider the deceased's reasons, including any reasons recorded in the will or a separate letter, but they are not bound by them. BC courts have repeatedly held that a parent's moral duty to provide for their children is a strong legal principle that subjective reasons cannot easily override.
What if the estate has already been distributed?
You may still have a claim, and in some circumstances the executor can be held personally liable for distributions made before claims were settled. Contact us immediately. The sooner you act, the better your position.
How much does it cost to challenge a will?
We offer free initial consultations and contingency fee arrangements on wills variation cases. On a contingency basis, you pay nothing upfront. Our fee comes from the recovery. If we don't recover, you owe nothing. Call to discuss the details of your specific situation.
What if my parent left everything to a new spouse?
Spouses have strong rights under WESA, but so do children. When there is a conflict between a surviving spouse's claim and a child's claim, courts balance both sets of rights. These cases are highly fact-specific and depend on the estate's size, the length of the marriage, and the children's financial circumstances. Call us to review your situation.
